The one sentence to anchor on

"I built the workflow a Forward Deployed Engineer actually ships — real machine telemetry into a triaged, evidence-backed recommendation, a mandatory human decision, and a write-back into the maintenance system of record — then I measured whether the AI part is trustworthy, on real data, and said out loud what the numbers don't prove."

If asked "is this enterprise-ready?"

"It's a production-shaped prototype. The architecture, the integration contract, the governance, and the evaluation discipline are the enterprise-grade parts. One asset runs on real recorded telemetry; the rest of the fleet is simulated for breadth. The datastore and the eval scale are deliberate stand-ins — each with a known, boring replacement I can name."

What the judges are really scoring

The rubric weights tell you where the points are: architecture 30%, engineering/SDLC 25%, enterprise integration 20%, domain 15%, agentic AI just 7%, governance/observability 3%. The model itself is the small slice. Two hard gates — miss either and polish won't save you:

  • A meaningful system integration — not a standalone script. → the bidirectional CMMS read + write-back through an anti-corruption layer.
  • Mandatory human accountability — nothing fully automatic. → a case cannot leave pending_review without a named human decision.

What the app actually does

A factory has machines; machines break; before they break their sensors show warning signs. This app watches those signs, works out what's probably going wrong, helps a human planner decide, and files the repair ticket in the factory's official maintenance system — with every step logged.

Words a judge might use

  • Predictive / condition-based maintenance: fix a machine before it fails, based on its measured condition — cheaper than a breakdown, less wasteful than a fixed calendar.
  • Unplanned downtime: the expensive event this whole thing exists to reduce — lost output, idle crews, rush parts.
  • Triage: deciding what to look at first and how urgently. A plant makes far more alerts than technicians can chase, so triage quality is the product.
  • Root cause vs symptom: the symptom is "vibration is high"; the root cause is "the suction line is starved."
  • Planner: the human who owns the ticket queue — in this app, the person who approves/rejects each case, by name.
  • CMMS: the plant's official maintenance software (real ones: SAP PM, IBM Maximo, Infor EAM). A mock one stands in here, reached only over HTTP.

The real-data story new in v2

The strongest single upgrade, and a genuine Forward-Deployed moment: one asset is not simulated at all.

PMP-03 replays real recorded telemetry from the SKAB pump testbed (Skoltech Anomaly Benchmark, GPL-3.0): physically induced rotor imbalance, cavitation, and inlet/outlet valve-restriction experiments, with the dataset authors' own per-row anomaly labels used as evaluation ground truth. It streams its real signal set — vibration acceleration (g), motor current (A), loop pressure (bar), motor & fluid temperature (°C), flow (L/min) — through the exact same detector and agent as the rest of the fleet. The "Inject fault" lever on this machine cues the actual recording just before its labelled window; nothing is synthesized.

Why this is the FDE story, not a footnote: real data broke a real assumption. The testbed runs at a different operating point in every recording (healthy flow is ~125 L/min in one run, ~32 in another), so a fixed threshold is blind there. That forced a second, still-deterministic detection rule — a robust z-score (median/MAD, |z|>4 sustained across 3 readings) measured against the machine's own rolling baseline. "I hit real-world messiness and the system had to adapt" is exactly what the role is about.

Honest scope: SKAB is a lab testbed, not a running plant, and it's one asset of nine. Say that plainly — "one asset is real telemetry; the rest are simulated for breadth." Don't imply the fleet is real.

The cavitation trap (still the best method story): on vibration alone, cavitation looks exactly like bearing wear — only the jumpy pressure/flow tells them apart. That's why the detector reports the shape of every signal, not just the one that breached (see the numbers section).

How it works — one step at a time

Every box is real code. The orange box is the human; the blue-outlined box is the real-data machine.

  • 1
    Machines stream telemetry 8 simulated machines on a tick, plus PMP-03 replaying real SKAB recordings. Each machine carries its own signal roster with units — like a real historian, not four fixed columns. (Code: simulator.py, replay.py)
    every few seconds
  • 2
    A rule-based detector watches — no AI Two deterministic rules: fixed engineering limits, and a robust z-score against the machine's own baseline (for assets whose normal shifts run to run). On a breach it summarizes how every signal is behaving — drift, volatility, range. Cheap, always explainable, checkable by hand. (Code: detector.py)
    opens a case for the AI
  • 3
    The AI agent investigates It uses three read-only tools — machine details, recent trend, and a search over past repair records — then writes a conclusion: likely cause, confidence, cited precedents, actions. The history search must now rank against routine records (PMs, false alarms), not just match keywords. (Code: agent/)
    conclusion becomes a case
  • 4
    A formula — not the AI — sets priority criticality + severity + recurrence + safety → P1–P4, shown openly. The AI may nudge ±1 with a written reason and can never downgrade a safety P1. The case also shows the dollar exposure (downtime × hourly cost) — informational, out of the score.
    case appears as "pending review"
  • 5
    A human planner decides — nothing happens without this Approve / Reject / Edit, with their name (taken from their signed-in session, not a free text box). Decisions are final and fully audited. The safety gate. (Code: routes.py, auth.py)
    only if approved
  • 6
    The adapter files the work order in the CMMS The anti-corruption layer translates the case into the CMMS's own language (priority code, ISO 14224 damage code, functional location) and sends it over HTTP — idempotent, retried with backoff. (Code: agent/cmms_adapter.py)
    result
  • 7
    The ticket sits in the system of record; every step is audited The CMMS holds the official work order; the audit trail recorded system → agent → human → system, each attributed. Now persisted in Postgres, so it survives redeploys.

Every module — and the one line to defend it

detector.py — rule-based detection, NOT the AI

Two rules: fixed limits and the robust z-score; on a breach, the per-signal signal context (drift/volatility/range for every signal).

defend"Detection is deterministic and quotable — 'vibration sustained 7.3 sigma above its own baseline for 3 readings.' I don't spend an AI call on arithmetic. And a breach names one signal, but the pattern across all of them names the fault."

replay.py — the real-data engine new

Streams curated SKAB episodes through the live pipeline; the dataset's labels ride along as eval ground truth; a warmup guards the baseline across episode boundaries so a regime change isn't mistaken for a fault.

defend"Same detector, same agent, real recorded physics. The 'inject' lever just cues the real recording — I never synthesize a real machine's fault."

auth.py — access gate + attribution new

One crew password gates every state-changing route; each reviewer signs in with their name, carried in an HMAC session token and stamped onto their decisions. Reading stays open so a shared link shows without acting.

defend"Accountability is only real if it's attributed and can't be spoofed — the decision is signed with the token's identity, not a name typed into a box."

llm_budget.py + the live/mock toggle new

Deployed default is the free mock policy; a header toggle (audited) switches new triage runs to the live model, under a hard daily call cap counted from the database. At the cap — or on any live-call failure — it degrades to mock and says so in the case trace.

defend"An always-on agent on a metered API is a cost-governance problem, so the cap is structural and the failure mode is graceful degradation, never a lost case."

agent/cmms_adapter.py — the anti-corruption layer

The only place the two vocabularies meet. Idempotent on the case id; retries 5xx with backoff; refuses to retry a 4xx (our bug) as a distinct terminal state.

defend · top integration point"Their format never leaks into my domain. The day the CMMS becomes real SAP PM, only this file and a URL change — and a retry can never double-raise a ticket."

eval/ — the evaluation harness

Drives the real pipeline against known answers — now including --data replay to score against the real SKAB labels. Two independent scorers cross-check the measuring tape.

defend · differentiator"Most candidates show a demo. I measured whether the AI is trustworthy, on real labels, and I can tell you exactly what the number doesn't prove."

Governance & controls (the 3% that signals seniority) new in v2

ControlWhat it does
Access gateState-changing actions require the crew password; viewing is open. No shared link can approve, inject, or toggle the model without signing in.
AttributionEvery decision, fault injection, and model toggle is signed with the reviewer's session identity in the append-only audit trail.
Spend capLive-LLM runs are hard-capped per day, counted from the DB (survives restarts); past the cap it degrades to mock.
Graceful degradationA live-model failure (bad key, outage, out of credit) falls back to the mock policy mid-case and records it in the trace — the case still reaches the planner.

Measured on the current pipeline in CI (live Sonnet 4.5, synthetic n=24, real n=16). On synthetic faults the LLM buys +12.5pp over the scripted baseline with excellent calibration (ECE 0.043). On real SKAB data it is the other way around: the LLM scores 31% vs the mock's 50% and its confidence is badly miscalibrated (ECE 0.398 — confidently wrong).

The headline finding — this is the most senior thing here

"The most valuable thing the eval told me is that the model's accuracy and its confidence don't transfer to real data it wasn't built around. On synthetic faults it looks great and well-calibrated; on the real pump it's worse than a keyword baseline and overconfident. That's exactly why the human gate is structural, not decorative — and exactly why you measure on real data before trusting a model in production. The harness catching this is the deliverable."

Caveat to volunteer: real n=16 (4/class), so −18.8pp is directional; the calibration blow-up (0.398 vs 0.043) is the robust signal and doesn't hinge on a few trials.

The method story — how the eval earns its keep

On the earlier synthetic-only pipeline, cavitation first scored 25% — every case called it bearing wear, yet the agent cited the cavitation work order every time. It had the precedent and no way to choose it: both faults show rising vibration and the detector only reported the breaching signal. No prompt fixes an absent signal. So I made the detector report drift/volatility/range for every signal — the shapes separate cleanly, and cavitation went 25% → 100%. Measure → find the real cause → fix the system → re-measure. That same loop is what just surfaced the real-data calibration gap above.

The measuring tape is itself cross-checked: two independent scorers (one reads the agent's free text, one reads its cited work-order ids) share no code, so their agreement rate audits the instrument — it caught two "agent mistakes" that were really harness bugs.

Weaknesses to volunteer yourself

  • The LLM underperforms on real data. +12.5pp on synthetic, but −18.8pp and badly overconfident on the real pump. I don't bury it — it's the finding that most justifies the human gate. (Real n=16 keeps it directional, not definitive.)
  • "Real data" is one machine deep, and SKAB is a lab testbed, not a plant. It proves the pipeline works on real physics; it doesn't prove plant-scale performance.
  • Small n — 40 synthetic trials, fewer per real class. The mock's own score swung 50→57.5% between runs with identical code; that's the noise floor.
  • Ground truth is still friendly — the fault is one the simulator/testbed seeded and the CMMS documents. "The harness is the deliverable; point it at real CMMS data and the loop is unchanged."
  • Keep-warm is best-effort — the free-tier ping usually keeps the link live, but I warm the URL before a demo anyway.

Live demo script (5–7 min)

Before you present: the backend is persistent now, but warm the URL anyway (hit /api/health, expect "ok":true). Sign in so you can act. Decide live vs mock: if the OpenRouter key is uncredited, stay on mock — the pill will read "LIVE unavailable — no key" otherwise, which is fine to show as honest governance but not what you want mid-flow.

  1. Frame it (20s): the one sentence above.
  2. Fleet (20s): the cards with live sparklines; point out PMP-03's ● real data badge and its real signal names.
  3. Open the real machine (20s): click PMP-03 → full-size charts of the real telemetry, its case history. "This one isn't simulated."
  4. Cue a fault (10s): on PMP-03, "Cue real fault" → rotor imbalance; or on a simulated machine, inject overheat. "The lever cues the real recording; normally these arise on their own."
  5. Open the case (~60s): walk the investigation-as-a-conversation — detector line → the signal-context table ("same numbers the agent saw; one signal breached, the pattern names the fault") → the hypothesis + cited precedents (safety flags in red, routine records marked) → the priority formula → the dollar exposure → the tool trace.
  6. Approve with your name. Show the CMMS work order on the case, then the CMMS tab (translated fields), then the audit trail — system → agent → human → system, each attributed.
  7. The trust question (mic-drop): "How do I know the AI is good?" → open the Evaluation page (confusion matrix live): "+12.5pp over the baseline on synthetic and well-calibrated — but on real data it's worse than the baseline and overconfident. That reversal is the most important thing I learned, and it's why the human gate is non-negotiable." Volunteer the small-n caveat.

Gotchas that look like bugs but are the design

  • Re-cueing the same fault can do nothing new — a cooldown dedups so one ongoing problem doesn't spam duplicate cases. If you cue a fault and no case appears, that's why; cue a different fault, or wait out the cooldown.
  • Cavitation only makes sense on pumps. Don't cue it on a compressor.
  • Live-AI wording varies — the eval scores meaning, not exact words.

Judge Q&A bank

⚔Isn't this just a wrapper around an API call?

The model is 7% of the rubric and one step. The work is the seams: two deterministic detection rules (one of which real operating-point variance forced), the signal-context computation that makes the hard faults separable, a transparent priority formula, a structural human gate with real attribution, a bidirectional CMMS integration through an anti-corruption layer, cost governance on the model, and an eval harness proving the AI is calibrated — on real labels. The API call is the easy part.

⚔Is any of this real, or all simulated?

One asset, PMP-03, is real recorded telemetry from the SKAB pump testbed — real induced faults, the dataset authors' labels, real signals. It's what forced the second detection rule. The other eight are simulated for breadth. I'm explicit about that split; I don't dress the fleet up as real.

Why two detection rules?

Fixed limits work when "normal" is fixed. The real testbed sits at a different operating point every run, so a fixed threshold is blind to a fault there. The robust z-score (median/MAD, sustained) catches a departure from the machine's own recent baseline — and median/MAD so the excursion doesn't poison the baseline it's measured against. Both are deterministic and quotable.

Why is priority a formula, not the AI's call?

Priority decides who gets dispatched — governance. It has to be an auditable rule a planner can read and contest. The AI gets exactly one notch of discretion, in writing, and can never downgrade a safety P1.

⚔What happens when you're out of LLM credit — like right now?

Exactly what you'd want in production: the live call fails, the pipeline falls back to the deterministic mock policy mid-case, records the fallback in the trace, and the case still reaches the planner. Nothing is lost, nothing hangs. That's the graceful-degradation path, and you can see it working in any case filed while the key is uncredited.

How do you know it works?

74 automated tests, plus the eval harness driving the real pipeline against known answers — 100% detection on both synthetic and real SKAB faults, a scripted baseline to measure the AI's lift against, calibrated confidence, and two cross-checking scorers. And I'll tell you what it doesn't prove.

How does this become production?

Swap the historian feed in behind the detector; point CMMS_BASE_URL at real SAP PM (only the adapter changes); SSO at the existing auth chokepoint; a job queue where the async hook already sits; run in shadow mode against real planners to earn trust before it writes anything. The architecture doesn't move.
